That three-point volume and efficiency gave them the exact comeback mechanism needed to beat a large live cushion.

### B. Houston’s shooting edge was real, but not enough to create separation
Houston still finished with:
- **132 points**
- **60.9% FG**
- **66.5% TS**

But they only made **4 threes** all game. Minnesota’s perimeter scoring advantage overwhelmed Houston’s better paint and rebounding profile.

### C. Turnover control mattered
- Minnesota turnovers: **7**
- Houston turnovers: **12**

Minnesota protected possessions well enough to convert offensive efficiency into scoreboard pressure. In a tight, high-scoring game, that was critical.

### D. Bench scoring swung the elasticity

These were the main failure paths:

## 8. Model Relevance Summary

This was a strong example of why LIVE-FLOW should not overreact to first-half efficiency edges when:
- the underdog is still within one or two possessions,
- the game has repeated lead changes,
- the favorite’s edge is driven by early shooting rather than structural control,
- and the underdog still has offensive scaling paths available.

**Takeaway:** In chaotic high-offense environments, plus-spreads on live underdogs can hold more value than the market implies, especially when the favorite’s early lead is built on efficiency that does not fully suppress opponent scoring pathways.
